Algorithmic Transparency in Criminal Sentencing: A Comparative Analysis

Sarah Johnson, Michael Chen | Stanford Law Review, 2026

📄 Empirical Legal Studies | 🔑 Algorithmic Accountability, Criminal Justice
Why this matters

Directly addresses the tension between proprietary algorithms and defendant rights—central to current debates on AI in criminal courts.

This paper examines how different jurisdictions balance commercial secrecy against due process in algorithmic risk assessment tools. The authors analyze 47 court cases across 12 countries, finding that courts increasingly demand algorithmic transparency while struggling to define standards. Key finding: jurisdictions with specialized AI tribunals show 34% higher compliance rates with transparency requirements.

02

Experimentalist Governance and Regulatory Learning: The Case of China's AI Filing System

Wei Zhang | Journal of Law and Political Economy, 2026

📄 Comparative Law | 🔑 Regulatory Innovation, Chinese Law, AI Governance
Why this matters>/div>

Offers rare empirical evidence on how "regulation by disclosure" actually functions at scale—relevant for policymakers considering similar approaches.

Using internal government documents and interviews with 23 regulators, this study reveals how China's 5,100 algorithm filings create a feedback loop between enforcement and policy adjustment. The "experimentalist" approach allows rapid policy iteration but raises questions about legal certainty. The paper proposes a framework for evaluating such systems' legitimacy under rule-of-law principles.

Does Deterrence Work? A Meta-Analysis of 40 Years of Empirical Research

Elena Rodriguez, James Park, et al. | Criminology, 2026

📄 Meta-Analysis | 🔑 Criminal Law, Deterrence, Sentencing Policy
Why this matters

Essential reading for sentencing policy debates. The findings challenge assumptions underlying many current criminal justice reforms.

This comprehensive meta-analysis of 312 studies finds that deterrence effects are highly context-dependent: certainty of punishment shows consistent effects (r=0.34), while severity effects are negligible (r=0.08). Most importantly, the study identifies a "tipping point" where excessive severity actually reduces compliance—suggesting optimal penalty ranges for policy design.

刑事量刑中的算法透明性：比较分析

Sarah Johnson, Michael Chen | 斯坦福法律评论, 2026

📄 实证法律研究 | 🔑 算法问责，刑事司法
为什么重要

直接针对专有算法与被告权利之间的张力——这是当前AI在刑事法庭应用辩论的核心问题。

本文考察不同司法管辖区如何在算法风险评估工具中平衡商业机密与正当程序。作者分析了12个国家的47个法庭案例，发现法院日益要求算法透明性，但难以界定标准。关键发现：设有专门AI法庭的司法管辖区，透明性要求的合规率高出34%。

实验主义治理与监管学习：中国AI备案系统的案例

张伟 | 法律与政治经济学杂志, 2026

📄 比较法 | 🔑 监管创新，中国法，AI治理
为什么重要

为"披露式监管"在大规模下如何实际运作提供了罕见的实证证据——对考虑类似方法的决策者具有参考价值。

利用政府内部文件和对23名监管者的访谈，本研究揭示了中国5,100项算法备案如何在执法与政策调整之间创建反馈循环。"实验主义"方法允许快速政策迭代，但引发了关于法律确定性的问题。论文提出了在法治原则下评估此类系统合法性的框架。

威慑有效吗？40年实证研究的元分析

Elena Rodriguez, James Park 等 | 犯罪学, 2026

📄 元分析 | 🔑 刑法，威慑，量刑政策
为什么重要

量刑政策辩论的必读文献。研究结果挑战了许多当前刑事司法改革背后的假设。

这项对312项研究的综合元分析发现，威慑效应高度依赖情境：惩罚的确定性显示一致效应(r=0.34)，而严厉性效应可忽略不计(r=0.08)。最重要的是，研究识别出过度严厉实际上会降低合规性的"临界点"——为政策设计提供了最优刑罚范围建议。
