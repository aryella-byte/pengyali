Fourth Amendment Equilibrium Adjustment in an Age of Technological Upheaval

Orin Kerr | Harvard Law Review, Vol. 139

📄 Harvard Law Review | 🔑 Fourth Amendment, Technology, Privacy
Why this matters

By Professor Orin Kerr, one of the country's foremost authorities on the Fourth Amendment and electronic privacy. His work has been deeply influential in shaping how courts decide issues raised by law enforcement use of technology.

Examines how courts should adapt Fourth Amendment doctrine to rapid technological change. The article proposes an equilibrium-adjustment framework for evaluating digital searches and surveillance.

Harvard Law Review | Vol. 139

📄 Harvard Law Review | 🔑 First Amendment, Social Media, Children

Addresses urgent questions about age-verification laws and their First Amendment implications as states seek to regulate minors' access to social media platforms.

Analyzes the constitutional standard for evaluating social media age-verification laws. Argues for intermediate scrutiny in the context of protecting minors while preserving free speech principles.

Ten Commandments Cases: Learning from Reformation Coercion

Michigan Law Review | Vol. 123

📄 Michigan Law Review | 🔑 Establishment Clause, Religion, History
Why this matters

Examines the Supreme Court's recent shift toward "historical practices and understandings" in Establishment Clause cases and its implications for religious displays in public spaces.

Critiques states' efforts to challenge precedent on Ten Commandments displays by exploring historical coercion during the Reformation. Questions whether originalist approaches adequately protect religious liberty.
